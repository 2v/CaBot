A 52-year-old man presented to the clinic with a six-week history of progressive shortness of breath and fatigue. He first noticed becoming winded while climbing the two flights of stairs to his apartment, something he had previously done without difficulty. Over the following weeks the breathlessness worsened, and he began waking at night feeling short of breath, finding relief only by sitting upright. He also reported mild swelling of both ankles that was most noticeable in the evenings.

His history was notable for hypertension, for which he took lisinopril irregularly, and he had smoked half a pack of cigarettes daily for roughly twenty years. He drank alcohol socially and worked as a warehouse supervisor. He reported no chest pain, fever, productive cough, or recent travel. A maternal uncle had reportedly died of a "heart problem" in his fifties.

On examination he was afebrile, with a blood pressure of 158/94 mm Hg, a heart rate of 104 beats per minute, a respiratory rate of 20 breaths per minute, and an oxygen saturation of 94% while breathing ambient air. The jugular venous pressure was elevated. Auscultation of the chest revealed bibasilar crackles, and a soft third heart sound was heard at the apex. There was pitting edema to the mid-shins bilaterally. The remainder of the examination was unremarkable.

Initial laboratory testing showed a mildly elevated serum creatinine and a markedly elevated B-type natriuretic peptide level. A chest radiograph demonstrated cardiomegaly with pulmonary vascular congestion and small bilateral pleural effusions. An electrocardiogram showed sinus tachycardia and voltage criteria for left ventricular hypertrophy without acute ischemic changes.

A diagnostic evaluation was undertaken.
